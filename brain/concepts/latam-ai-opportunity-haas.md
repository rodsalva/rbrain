## Compiled Truth

### The Market Size

Latin America is the **third largest economic block in the world**:
- 650 million people (vs. 330M in the US)
- Abundant assets: engineering talent, GPUs, energy, commodities, biodiversity
- Two oceans, similar cultures, similar religions, largely aligned time zones
- Three primary languages (Spanish, Portuguese, English) with strong institutional overlap

Despite this, per capita growth has been flat for a decade. The reason: the cost of doing business (compliance, regulatory, low-trust environment) consumes resources that should go toward productive investment.

### The "Humans as a Service" (HaaS) Problem

The core reason SaaS never took off in LATAM: **labor was cheaper than software.**

> "I remember trying to sell fraud software. They'd say: 'Why would I do that? I've got 200 people that cost less.'" — a16z investor

The entire enterprise stack in Latin America was built on HaaS:
- DP (Departamento Pessoal) teams of dozens managing compliance manually
- Customer service via call centers instead of self-service software
- Fraud detection via human review teams
- Document processing via data entry operators

**The consequence:** Entire industries are 15-20 years behind in software adoption. The "SaaS generation" was skipped.

### The Skip-Generation AI Opportunity

This creates a **double opportunity** for AI companies in LATAM:
1. Companies aren't replacing SaaS with AI — they're going directly from HaaS to AI agents
2. The total addressable market includes both the software market AND the labor market

There are massive service sectors in LATAM that are:
- Low-quality (humans doing boring, error-prone work)
- High-cost at scale (armies of DP, legal, compliance staff)
- Ripe for AI displacement

The specific markets Sebas identifies:
- **Labor/HR compliance**: $20-30B in friction in Brazil alone
- **Tax compliance**: similar magnitude
- **Legal/regulatory advisory**: historically required expensive specialists
- **Logistics**: Rappi proved this with owned delivery infrastructure

### Why No Global AI Winners Have Emerged from LATAM Yet

As of 2025/2026:
- The large AI companies scaling globally haven't come out of LATAM yet
- Reasons: complex regulatory environment slows iteration; global talent concentration in SF/NYC; underfunded AI research locally; underinvestment by US companies in LATAM offices (2 people in a Brazil office is typical)
- But: the companies being built now are well-positioned because they understand both the local complexity AND the global AI stack

### The Sovereignty Risk

Sebas raises a structural concern: **China is quietly dominating LATAM's AI infrastructure stack:**

- **Telco:** ~100% of Mexico's telco infrastructure is Huawei. This is not 99% — it's 100%.
- **Energy:** Brazil-China is the largest bilateral trading relationship; energy exports go to China
- **Applications:** Chinese apps increasingly penetrating LATAM consumer markets
- **Models:** No Chinese open-source models are culturally aligned with LATAM

The risk: if LATAM's AI stack runs on Chinese infrastructure, it's not controllable by LATAM or aligned with democratic/American values.

**The prescription:**
- Build AI clusters in LATAM with American technology (not Chinese)
- Develop open-source models trained on culturally-aligned LATAM data
- US companies need to take LATAM offices seriously (not 2-person satellite offices)
- LATAM founders need to fine-tune their own models for their use cases

### What It Takes to Win

From Rappi's experience building where no stack existed:
- **Go vertical.** Own logistics, payments, fraud, compliance. Don't rely on "as-a-service" that doesn't exist.
- **Embrace the complexity.** Hard markets are where durable moats are built.
- **Hire globally.** The bar has to be globally great, not locally great. Rappi recruited from DoorDash, Uber, etc.
- **Think about defensibility early.** Network effects, scale, switching costs, hard IP.
- **Build durably.** The book *Engines That Move Markets* shows every technology revolution was won by companies with monopoly-like durability, not just innovation speed.

### The Corridor Vision

Sebas's thesis: the Americas should function as an integrated economic corridor:
- Talent flowing between US and LATAM
- Companies operating bi-directionally (LATAM-founded companies should be able to scale into US; US companies should invest deeply in LATAM)
- Capital flowing in both directions
- AI infrastructure owned by the hemisphere, not external powers
