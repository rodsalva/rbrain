## Compiled Truth

### The Framework

Winston Weinberg (Harvey CEO) articulates the most important mental model for application-layer AI companies: **assume the LLM providers will enter your vertical**. This is not a risk to be managed probabilistically — it should be treated as a certainty.

> "Just assume they're going to directly go into your vertical. Just no matter what. Just assume that they're going to. How would you defend against that and on what time scale?"

**The framework has two levels:**

**Level 1 — Value Capture Floor:** Your platform must be meaningfully better than raw access to the same underlying model. If `your product ≈ ChatGPT + correct prompt`, you have no moat. This is the minimum hygiene check.

**Level 2 — Sherlock Defense:** Model your largest indirect competitor entering your vertical directly. Think: what advantages would they have? How would they get mass distribution? What would they build first? Then design your moat against *that* scenario — not against other application-layer startups.

### Why This Matters More Than Competing With Startups

- LLM providers (OpenAI, Anthropic) are the **real long-term competitive threat** for any application-layer company.
- Other startups in your space are secondary. The foundation model providers can commoditize them all simultaneously.
- Most founders optimize their strategy against horizontal competitors (other legal AI startups, etc.) rather than against the vertical entry of the LLM provider.

### Harvey's Structural Moats (Examples)

1. **Context complexity** — Legal M&A work has context spread across many systems, years of precedent, jurisdiction-specific rules. Getting all of that context right requires domain-specific pipeline engineering. A generic chatbot can't do this even with a perfect model.

2. **Enterprise relationships + data flywheel** — Hundreds of thousands of enterprise users providing domain-specific feedback. The signal density creates a short-term moat that's hard to replicate quickly.

3. **GTM deliberately targets the top of the market** — Harvey avoided low-end legal work specifically because it's more easily commoditized by foundation models. This sacrificed early revenue but protects long-term positioning.

4. **Market data** — Harvey has enough data from law firms AND corporate clients to know how the market thinks about legal work. This is a trust/credibility moat in enterprise sales.

5. **Adjacent verticals** — Tax and compliance teams at enterprise clients naturally adopt Harvey after legal (similar workflow, not technically lawyers). This diversifies the moat beyond legal-specific model improvements.

### The GTM Corollary

- Building for the low-end of any market makes you more vulnerable to sherlock. Foundation models with broad distribution can commoditize low-end work quickly.
- Starting at the top (largest firms, most complex use cases) creates a moat through complexity and relationship depth that's harder to replicate.
- Tradeoff: slower early revenue growth. Winston explicitly says this was a conscious choice that "probably slowed down our revenue growth by a lot."

### Rapper Company Evolution

The term "wrapper company" (derogatory ~2023) has evolved. As of 2025:
- Enterprise clients have both ChatGPT/Claude AND domain-specific apps like Harvey — co-existence is the norm.
- The "wrapper" fear was partly a distribution problem: enterprises initially just bought Azure OpenAI instances and built generic chatbots. As domain-specific apps proved value, the narrative shifted.
- Genuine moats ARE buildable at the application layer — they just require deliberate, long-term bets that trade short-term gains for defensibility.

### Related Mental Models

- **Jevons Paradox in professional services:** Making lawyers faster doesn't reduce total legal work — it increases it (more complexity, more deals, same people). AI amplifies capacity.
- **Navy Seals + Scale:** Companies need both nimble high-skill squads (Navy Seals) AND disciplined scaled systems. Over-indexing either way leads to failure at different stages.
